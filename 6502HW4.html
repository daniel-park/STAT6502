11.1

A computer was used to generate four random numbers from a normal distribution with a set mean and variance: \(1.1650, 0.6268, 0.0751, 0.3516\). Five more random normal numbers with the same variance but perhaps a different mean were then generated (the mean may or may not actually be different): \(0.3035, 2.6961, 1.0591, 2.7971, 1.2641\).

  1. What do you think the means of the random normal number generators were? What do you think the difference of the means was?
    Solution
    Relying on the MLE for the mean of sample observations from a normal distribution, the means and differences are \[ \begin{aligned} \hat{\mu}_X &= \overline{X} = \frac{1}{4}(1.1650 + 0.6268 + 0.0751 + 0.3516) = 0.554625 \\ \hat{\mu}_Y &= \overline{Y} = \frac{1}{5}(0.3035 + 2.6961 + 1.0591 + 2.7971 + 1.2641) = 1.62398 \\ \hat{\mu}_X - \hat{\mu}_Y &= \overline{X} - \overline{Y} = 0.554625 - 1.62398 = -1.069355 \end{aligned} \]

  2. What do you think the variance of the random number generator was?
    Solution
    The variances, which are the same for the two sets, must be estimated by calculating the pooled sample variance, \[ s^2_p = \frac{(n-1)s_X^2 + (m-1)s_Y^2}{m+n-2} \] where \(s^2_X = \frac{1}{n-1} \sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \overline{X})^2\). Then we have \[ \begin{aligned} s^2_X &= \frac{1}{3}\Big[(1.1650-0.5546)^2 + (0.6268 - 0.5546)^2 + (0.0751 - 0.5546)^2 + (0.3516-0.5546)^2\Big] = 0.2163101 \\ s^2_Y &= \frac{1}{4}\Big[(0.3035-1.6240)^2 + (2.6961 - 1.6240)^2 + (1.0591 - 1.6240)^2 + (2.7971-1.6240)^2 + (1.2641-1.6240)^2\Big] = 1.1794806 \end{aligned} \] and then \[ s^2_p = \frac{(3)0.2163101 + (4)1.1794806}{7} = 0.7666932 \]

  3. What is the estimated standard error of your estimate of the difference of the means?
    Solution
    Estimated standard error of \(\overline{X} - \overline{Y}\): \[ \begin{aligned} s_{\overline{X}-\overline{Y}} &=s_p\sqrt{\frac{1}{n}+\frac{1}{m}} \\ &= 0.8756102 \sqrt{\frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{5}} \\ &= 0.5873772 \end{aligned} \]
  4. Form a 90% confidence interval for the difference of the means of the random number generators.
    Solution
    A \(100(1 - \alpha)\%\) confidence interval for \(\mu_X - \mu_Y\) is \[ (\overline{X} - \overline{Y}) \pm t_{m+n-2, \ \alpha/2}s_{(\overline{X}-\overline{Y})} \] where \(t_{(m+n-2,\alpha/2)}\)=qt(p=0.95, df=7)=1.8945786. \[ -1.069355 \pm 1.8945786\cdot 0.5873772 \ \ \ \Longrightarrow \ \ \ -2.1821872 \geq \mu_X - \mu_Y \geq 0.0434772 \]

  5. In this situation, is it more appropriate to use a one-sided test or a two-sided test of the equality of the means?
    Solution
    We should use a two-sided test. We are not told how the two sets of observations differ from each other. If we knew if one sample was either equal to or greater than a sample, then a one-sided test would be more appropriate.

  6. What is the p-value of a two-sided test of the null hypothesis of equal means?
    Solution
    The test statistic is \[ \begin{aligned} t &= \frac{\overline{X} - \overline{Y}}{s_{(\overline{X} - \overline{Y})}} \\ &= \frac{-1.069355}{0.5873772} \\ &= -1.8205593 \end{aligned} \] The p-value is 2*pt(q=test.stat, df=n+m-2)=0.1114772.
    Or we may use t.test() function: t.test(x=x, y=y, var.equal=TRUE)$p.value: 0.1114772.

  7. Would the hypothesis that the means were the same versus a two-sided alternative be rejected at the significance level \(\alpha=0.1\)?
    Solution
    The p-value indicates that the test statistic falls within the acceptance region if the significance level were \(\alpha=0.1\). So the null hypothesis is not rejected.

  8. Suppose you know that the variance of the normal distribution was \(\sigma^2=1\). How would your answers to the preceding questions change?
    Solution
    The t-distribution is used when the population variance is unknown. When it is known, we may use the normal distribution.

    1. The means and their difference would be the same.
    2. The variance of the random generator is given: \(\sigma^2=1\).
    3. When variance is known we know that \[ \overline{X} - \overline{Y} \sim N\bigg[\mu_X - \mu_Y, \sigma^2\bigg(\frac{1}{n} + \frac{1}{m}\bigg)\bigg] \] So the standard deviation is is \[ 1\sqrt{\frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{5}} = 0.6708204 \]
    4. The confidence interval is \[ (\overline{X} - \overline{Y}) \pm z_{(\alpha/2)}\sigma\sqrt{\frac{1}{n} + \frac{1}{m}} = -2.1727564, 0.0340464 \]
    5. Again, a two-sided test is more appropriate.
    6. The Z value is \[ Z=\frac{(\overline{X} - \overline{Y}) - (\mu_X - \mu_Y)}{\sigma^2\sqrt{\frac{1}{n} + \frac{1}{m}}} = -1.5941003 \] The p-value is 0.1109136.
    7. Our test statistic falls in the acceptance region. Again, we fail to reject.

11.2

The difference of the means of two normal distrbutions with equal variance is to be estimated by sampling an equal number of observations from each distribution. If it were possible, would it be better to halve the standard deviations of the populations or double the sample sizes?

Solution
Assuming that the variance is unknown (though equal), our test statistic is dependent on estimated standard error: \[ t = \frac{\overline{X} - \overline{Y}}{s_{(\overline{X} - \overline{Y})}} \] where \(s_{(\overline{X} - \overline{Y})}=s_p\sqrt{\frac{1}{n} + \frac{1}{m}}= s_p\sqrt{\frac{2}{n}}\) A smaller pooled standard error would be beneficial since it would magnify the value of the t-statistic.

Comparing the halving of the standard error vs.doubling the sample sizes, \[ \begin{aligned} \bigg(\frac{1}{2}s_p\bigg)\sqrt{\frac{2}{n}} \ \ \ &? \ \ \ s_p\sqrt{\frac{2}{(2n)}} \\ s_p\sqrt{\frac{2}{4n}} \ \ \ &? \ \ \ s_p\sqrt{\frac{1}{n}} \\ s_p\sqrt{\frac{1}{2n}} \ \ \ &< \ \ \ s_p\sqrt{\frac{1}{n}} \end{aligned} \] So it is better to halve the standard error.

11.3

In Section 11.2.1, we considered two methods of estimating Var\((\overline{X} - \overline{Y})\). Under the assumption that the two population variances were equal, we estimated this quantity by \[ s^2_p\bigg(\frac{1}{n} + \frac{1}{m}\bigg) \] and without this assumption by \[ \frac{s_X^2}{n} + \frac{s_Y^2}{m} \] Show that these two estimates are identical if \(m=n\).
Solution
The pooled variance is \[ s^2_p = \frac{(n-1)s_X^2 + (m-1)s_Y^2}{m+n-2} \] Substituting \(s^2_p\) into the first equation and letting \(\ell=m=n\), we have \[ \begin{aligned} s^2_p\bigg(\frac{1}{n} + \frac{1}{m}\bigg) &= \frac{(n-1)s_X^2 + (m-1)s_Y^2}{m+n-2} \bigg(\frac{1}{n} + \frac{1}{m}\bigg) \\ &= \frac{(\ell-1)s_X^2 + (\ell-1)s_Y^2}{\ell+\ell-2} \bigg(\frac{1}{\ell} + \frac{1}{\ell}\bigg) \\ &= \frac{(\ell-1)s_X^2 + (\ell-1)s_Y^2}{2(\ell-1)} \bigg(\frac{2}{\ell}\bigg) \\ &= \frac{s_X^2}{\ell} + \frac{s_Y^2}{\ell} \\ &= \frac{s_X^2}{n} + \frac{s_Y^2}{m} \end{aligned} \]

11.15

Suppose that \(n\) measurements are to be taken under a treatment condition and another \(n\) measurements are to be taken independently under a control condition. It is thought that the standard deviation of a single observation is about 10 under both conditions. How large should \(n\) be so that a \(95\%\) confidence interval for \(\mu_X-\mu_Y\) has a width of \(2\)? Use the normal distribution rather than the \(t\) distribution, since \(n\) will turn out to be rather large.
Solution
Under a normal distribution, the confidence interval is \[ (\overline{X} - \overline{Y}) \pm z_{(\alpha /2)}\sigma \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} + \frac{1}{m}} \] In this case, \(n=m\). To calculate the sample size to ensure a width of 2, we may utilize the normal distributions symmetry to simplify the derivation: \[ \begin{aligned} 1 &= z_{(\alpha /2)}\sigma \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} + \frac{1}{m}} \\ \frac{1}{z_{(\alpha /2)}\sigma} &= \sqrt{\frac{2}{n}} \\ \bigg(\frac{1}{z_{(\alpha /2)}\sigma}\bigg)^2 &= \frac{2}{n} \\ 2\big(z_{(\alpha /2)}\sigma\big)^2 &= n \\ \end{aligned} \] Given our values, we can plug them in \[ \begin{aligned} n &= 2\big(z_{(0.975)}\sigma\big)^2 \\ &= 2\big(1.96 \cdot 10\big)^2 \\ &= 768.32 \end{aligned} \] To ensure a 95% confidence interval with a width of 2, the sample size should be \(769\).

11.16

Referring to Problem 15, how large should be \(n\) be so that the test of \(H_0: \mu_X=\mu_Y\) against the one-sided alternative \(H_A:\mu_x > \mu_Y\) has a power of \(0.5\) if \(\mu_X-\mu_Y=2\) and \(\alpha =0.10\)?

Solution
The rejection region for a one-sided test is \[ \overline{X} - \overline{Y} > z_{(\alpha)} \sigma \sqrt{\frac{2}{n}} \] The power is the probability that the test statistic falls in the rejection region: \[ \text{Power} = P\bigg[\overline{X} - \overline{Y} > z_{(\alpha)} \sigma \sqrt{\frac{2}{n}}\bigg] \] We know that \[ \overline{X} - \overline{Y} \sim N\bigg[\mu_Y - \mu_X, \sigma^2 \frac{2}{n}\bigg] \] Our goal is to manipulate the probability so that it follows a standard normal distribution \[ \begin{aligned} \overline{X} - \overline{Y} &> z_{(\alpha)} \sigma \sqrt{\frac{2}{n}} \\ (\overline{X} - \overline{Y}) - (\mu_X - \mu_Y) &> z_{(\alpha)} \sigma \sqrt{\frac{2}{n}} - (\mu_X - \mu_Y) \\ \frac{(\overline{X} - \overline{Y}) - (\mu_X - \mu_Y)}{\sigma\sqrt{2/n}} &> \frac{z_{(\alpha)} \sigma \sqrt{2/n} - (\mu_X - \mu_Y)}{\sigma \sqrt{2/n}} \\ Z &> \frac{z_{(\alpha)} \sigma \sqrt{2/n}}{\sigma \sqrt{2/n}} - \frac{(\mu_X - \mu_Y)}{\sigma \sqrt{2/n}} \\ Z &> z_{(\alpha)} - \frac{(\mu_X - \mu_Y)}{\sigma \sqrt{2/n}} \\ \end{aligned} \] So returning back to the probability \[ \begin{aligned} \text{Power} &= P\bigg[\overline{X} - \overline{Y} > z_{(\alpha)} \sigma \sqrt{\frac{2}{n}}\bigg] \\ &= P\bigg[Z > z_{(\alpha)} - \frac{(\mu_X - \mu_Y)}{\sigma \sqrt{2/n}}\bigg] \\ &= 1 - \Phi\bigg[z_{(\alpha)} - \frac{(\mu_X - \mu_Y)}{\sigma \sqrt{2/n}}\bigg] \end{aligned} \] Plug in our known values \[ \begin{aligned} 0.5 &= 1 - \Phi\bigg[z_{(0.10)} - \frac{2}{10 \sqrt{2/n}}\bigg] \\ \Phi\bigg[1.28 - \frac{1}{5 \sqrt{2/n}}\bigg] &= 0.5 \end{aligned} \] Since 0.5 is the median we know that \(\Phi(0)=0.5\). So \[ \begin{aligned} 0 &= 1.28 - \frac{1}{5 \sqrt{2/n}} \\ n &= 81.92 \end{aligned} \] So \(n\) should be at least \(82\).

  1. What will the standard error of \(\overline{Y}-\overline{X}\) be?
    Solution
    Given our scenario of two sets of samples from normally distributed populations with known and equal variance, \[ \overline{Y} - \overline{X} \sim N\bigg[\mu_Y - \mu_X, \sigma^2 \bigg(\frac{1}{n} + \frac{1}{m}\bigg)\bigg] \] So the standard error is \[ \begin{aligned} \sqrt{\sigma^2\bigg(\frac{1}{n} + \frac{1}{m}\bigg)} &= 5\sqrt{\frac{1}{25} + \frac{1}{25}} \\ &= \sqrt{2} \end{aligned} \]

11.32

If \(X \sim N(\mu_X, \sigma_X^2)\) and \(Y\) is independent \(N(\mu_Y, \sigma_Y^2)\), what is \(\pi=P(X<Y)\) in terms of \(\mu_X, \mu_Y, \sigma_X,\) and \(\sigma_Y\)?
Solution
We know that the sum/difference of random variables from a normal distribution also follow a normal distribution. In this case \[ Y-X \sim N(Y-X, \sigma^2_Y + \sigma^2_X) \] Then converting \(Y-X\) into a standard normal distribution: \[ \frac{(Y-X) - (\mu_Y - \mu_X)}{\sigma^2_Y + \sigma^2_X} \sim N(0,1) \] So then we have: \[ \begin{aligned} P(X<Y) &= P(Y-X > 0) \\ &= P[(Y-X) - (\mu_Y - \mu_X) > 0 - (\mu_Y - \mu_X)] \\ &= P\bigg(\frac{(Y-X) - (\mu_Y - \mu_X)}{\sqrt{\sigma^2_Y + \sigma^2_X}} > \frac{ - (\mu_Y - \mu_X)}{\sqrt{\sigma^2_Y + \sigma^2_X}}\bigg) \\ &= P\bigg(Z > \frac{ - (\mu_Y - \mu_X)}{\sqrt{\sigma^2_Y + \sigma^2_X}}\bigg) \\ &= 1 - \Phi\bigg(\frac{ - (\mu_Y - \mu_X)}{\sqrt{\sigma^2_Y + \sigma^2_X}}\bigg) \end{aligned} \]
